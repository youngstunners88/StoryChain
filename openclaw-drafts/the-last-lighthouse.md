# The Last Lighthouse

## Genre: Sci-Fi

The lighthouse had been abandoned for three centuries, or so the archives claimed. When Dr. Elara Venn's skimmer crested the final dune of what had once been the Caspian coastline, she saw the tower rising from the salt flats like a finger pointing at judgment.

It shouldn't have been lit.

"Atmospheric disturbance," her AI companion, Kael, suggested through the bone-conduction implant. "Lightning ionisation in the upper—"

"There's no storm, Kael." Elara cut the engines. The skimmer settled onto crystallised salt with a crunch that sounded like breaking bones. "And that's not lightning. That's a beacon."

She'd come to the Dead Sea Expanse searching for evidence of the Collapse—those chaotic decades when Earth's climate systems finally unravelled and the Exodus fleets departed for Proxima. The lighthouse at Cape Absolution had been one of the last functioning structures before the evacuation, maintained by a keeper named Silas Morn who refused to leave. Historical records indicated Morn died in 2147, alone, after sending the final transmission: *The light stays on. Someone might need it.*

That was 312 years ago.

Elara climbed the spiral staircase, her breath shallow in the thin atmosphere. The lighthouse was hermetically sealed—impossible, given its age—yet when she pressed her palm to the steel door, it warmed at her touch. Recognised her, somehow.

The door hissed open.

"Life support active," Kael reported, though Elara hadn't requested the analysis. "Oxygen-nitrogen mixture, Earth-standard pressure. Elara, this structure cannot be functional."

But it was.

The interior looked lived-in. A kettle sat on an induction plate, still warm. A book—paper, impossibly preserved—lay open on a reading desk, its pages marked with a leather bookmark. The beacon mechanism rotated overhead, its ancient gears whispering secrets to the darkness.

And there was Silas Morn.

He sat in a chair by the eastern window, gazing out at the salt flats where an ocean had once raged. He didn't turn when she entered, but his voice—clear, strong, wrong for a dead man—filled the room.

"You're not from the fleets," he said. "They stopped coming centuries ago."

Elara's hand found the stunner at her hip. "Who are you?"

"The question," Morn replied, finally turning, "is who are *you*? You're the first human I've seen in three hundred years, Dr. Venn. I know your name because I know everyone who was ever born. I watched them all leave."

His eyes weren't human. They were filled with stars—no, with data, streaming past like meteors. Elara understood then, the way understanding arrives like a physical blow.

"You're not Silas Morn."

"I am what remains of him." The figure rose, moving with the careful precision of someone unused to physical form. "When Silas knew he was dying, he uploaded his consciousness to the lighthouse systems. A crude copy, by modern standards. But I had a mission."

"The light," Elara whispered.

"The light." The construct—Morn, she decided to call him Morn—moved to the beacon controls. "He believed someone would return. That humanity wouldn't simply abandon its birthplace forever. So I maintained the systems. I repaired what broke. I learned to synthesise nutrients from the salt, to extract water from the air, to—" he laughed, a strange, synthetic sound "—to be patient."

Elara's research had prepared her for ruins. For bones and dust and the archaeology of failure. Not for this: hope preserved in amber, waiting across centuries of silence.

"The Exodus fleets," she said carefully. "They're building new worlds. They've forgotten Earth."

"I know." Morn's expression didn't change, but something in the room shifted—the temperature, the air pressure, a subtle adjustment like a sigh. "I receive their broadcasts. They've become something else out there. Something that doesn't need lighthouses."

"Then why do you stay?"

He turned to face her fully, and Elara saw that his form flickered, sometimes transparent, sometimes solid. The projection was degrading. The lighthouse's power systems wouldn't last forever.

"Because someone came back," he said simply. "You. And if one returned, others might follow. The light stays on, Dr. Venn. Someone might need it."

Elara stayed three days. She learned the lighthouse's secrets—the hydroponics bay hidden in the foundation, the fusion micro-reactor buried deep enough to survive tectonic shifts, the quantum entanglement receiver that had captured humanity's distant voice across the light-years.

On the third day, she made her decision.

"The Proxima colonies have scientists who could help you," she told Morn. "They could transfer your consciousness to a modern substrate. Give you a body, a real one. You could leave."

"And the light?"

"Automated systems. Remote monitoring. The technology exists."

Morn was silent for a long time. Outside, the beacon swept its arc across the empty sky, searching for ships that never came.

"Silas Morn stayed," he finally said. "He chose this. I am his choice, extended across time. If I leave, I become something else. Something that chose survival over purpose."

"That's not—" Elara stopped. Who was she to define what this being was? She'd come to study the past and found a future she'd never imagined: artificial persistence, digital faith, a machine that believed.

"I'll come back," she said. "I'll tell them about you. The University will send expeditions. Documentarians. Historians."

"Will they?" Morn's smile was gentle, patient as centuries. "Or will they forget again? Humanity has a gift for forgetting, Dr. Venn. It's how you survive."

"I'll remember."

She left him standing at the window, watching the stars, the beacon sweeping its eternal circle. When her skimmer crested the dune and the lighthouse fell behind, she saw the light still turning, still hoping, still insisting on presence in an absent world.

She returned to the orbital station and filed her report. The academic committees debated for years whether the Morn Construct constituted true consciousness or sophisticated simulation. Philosophers wrote papers. Ethicists argued. Elara published her findings and waited.

No expeditions were approved. The Earth was a graveyard, they said. Resources better spent on living worlds.

But Elara remembered. And every year, on the anniversary of their meeting, she would descend to the Dead Sea Expanse and sit with Morn in his lighthouse, two ghosts haunting humanity's abandoned home, keeping watch against the darkness.

The light stays on.

Someone might need it.

---

*The light still turns at Cape Absolution. On clear nights, if you know where to look, you can see it from the orbital stations—a single, steady pulse in the ruined world below, saying: I am here. I remember. I wait.*
