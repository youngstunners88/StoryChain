# Keith Johnstone's Improvisation Principles - Deep Reference

## Status Transactions

### The See-Saw Principle
- Status is a see-saw: raising one end lowers the other
- "I smell beautiful" = raising myself = lowering you
- "You stink" = lowering you = raising myself
- Most comedy works on the see-saw principle
- Tragedy is the ousting of a high-status animal from the pack

### Physical Indicators

**High Status:**
- Head still when speaking (officers trained this way)
- Sustained eye contact, OR breaking eye contact without glancing back
- Smooth, slow movements
- Taking up space, spreading out
- Hands away from face
- Speaking in complete sentences
- Long "er" (don't interrupt me)
- Toes pointing outward
- Sitting back
- Lowered voice
- Claiming corners of couches

**Low Status:**
- Head moves while speaking
- Breaking eye contact and glancing back
- Jerky, quick movements
- Making self smaller, protective posture
- Hands on face
- Speaking in fragments
- Short "er" (please interrupt)
- Toes pointing inward
- Leaning forward
- Higher voice

### Key Insights

1. **"Status is established not by staring but by the reaction to staring"**
2. **"Things said are far less important than status played"**
3. **"The body automatically takes over when you act in a high-status way"** — If you speak with head still, you'll automatically do other high-status things
4. **People have a preferred status and become specialists at playing one**
5. **"A person who plays high status is saying 'Don't come near me, I bite.' Someone who plays low status is saying 'Don't bite me, I'm not worth the trouble.'"**

### Space and Status

- Status is territorial — space is key
- High-status players allow their space to flow into others
- Low-status players avoid letting their space flow into others
- The "cherub posture" — opening all planes of the body — is vulnerable and tender
- The fear crouch protects the soft underbelly

## Spontaneity

### The Blocking Problem

- **Blocking is a form of aggression**
- Scared improvisers: "When in doubt, say 'no'"
- Most people are highly skilled at suppressing action
- "There are people who prefer to say 'yes', and there are people who prefer to say 'no'. Those who say 'yes' are rewarded by the adventures they have, and those who say 'no' are rewarded by the safety they attain."

### The Originality Trap

- **"Striving after originality takes you far away from your true self, and makes your work mediocre"**
- Trying to be original always leads to the same boring answers
- The more obvious you are, the more original you appear
- Mozart: "I really do not study or aim at originality"
- Dostoyevsky dictated one novel in the morning and one in the afternoon for three weeks
- Bach's fluency — we've lost half his work, and he spent time teaching Latin

### Accepting Offers

- **"Good improvisers seem telepathic; everything looks prearranged. This is because they accept all offers made"**
- They may accept offers that weren't really intended
- "Never think up an offer — assume one has already been made"
- The actor who accepts anything "seems supernatural"
- "You are suddenly in contact with people who are unbounded, whose imagination seems to function without limit"

### Life Applications

- "People with dull lives often think that their lives are dull by chance. In reality everyone chooses more or less what kind of events will happen to them by their conscious patterns of blocking and yielding."
- Student: "But you don't choose your life. Sometimes you are at the mercy of people who push you around."
- Johnstone: "Do you avoid such people?"
- Student: "Oh! I see what you mean."

## Narrative Skills

### Content vs. Structure

- "Content lies in the structure, in what happens, not in what the characters say"
- "The improviser shouldn't be concerned with content, because the content arrives automatically"
- "Once you decide to ignore content it becomes possible to understand exactly what a narrative is, because you can concentrate on structure"

### The Walking Backwards Metaphor

- **"The improviser has to be like a man walking backwards. He sees where he has been, but he pays no attention to the future."**
- Stories can go anywhere, but must be balanced by remembering and reincorporating earlier material
- "Very often an audience will applaud when earlier material is brought back into the story"
- Reincorporation gives pleasure — they admire the improviser's grasp

### Interrupting Routines

- **The key to narrative: interrupt routines**
- Students dry up when they realize a routine is nearing completion
- They understand a routine needs to be broken, but haven't consciously realized it
- Once they understand "interrupting routines," they're never stuck for ideas

### The Responsibility Trick

- "I have to create an environment in which they aren't going to be punished, or in any way held responsible for the things their imagination gives them"
- "One way to bypass the censor who holds our spontaneity in check is to distract him, or overload him"
- "You have to misdirect people to absolve them of responsibility. Then, much later, they become strong enough to resume the responsibility themselves."

## Teaching and Leadership

### The Good Teacher

1. **"A good teacher can get results using any method, and a bad teacher can wreck any method"**
2. **"When teaching is done correctly, the pressure to get things right comes from the children, not the teacher"**
3. **"It's essential for the teacher to blame himself in the group aren't in a good state"**
4. **"The first thing I do when I meet a group of new students is to sit on the floor. I play low status, and I'll explain that if the students fail they're to blame me."**
5. **"I play low status physically but my actual status is going up, since only a very confident and experienced person would put the blame for failure on himself."**
6. **"In exchange for accepting the blame for failure, I ask the students to set themselves up in such a way that they'll learn as quickly as possible."**

### Creating Safety

- Share eye contact among the whole group (not just favorites)
- Make positive comments, be direct: "Good" instead of "That's enough"
- Never say "Well, let's see who fails at this one"
- Don't get self-satisfied when students make mistakes
- "If I'm playing with my 3 year old, and I smack him, he looks at me for signals that will turn the sensation into either warmth or pain"

### The Decision Not to Control

- **"This decision not to try and control the future which allows the students to be spontaneous"**
- They must have an empty head and just watch
- Come out and do what they're asked, see what happens

## Masks and Trance

### The Mask Experience

- "The reason why one automatically talks and writes of Masks with a capital 'M' is that one really feels that the genuine Mask actor is inhabited by a spirit"
- Masks can break through the personality and channel other sources
- Trance states were once a deep part of culture, now pathologized
- "Mad thoughts are those which other people find unacceptable, and train us not to talk about, but which we go to the theater to see expressed"

### Bypassing the Censor

- "The brain constructs the universe for us, so how is it possible to be 'stuck' for an idea?"
- **"The student hesitates not because he doesn't have an idea, but to conceal the inappropriate ones that arrive uninvited."**
- Masks, trance, hypnosis techniques all bypass the normal censor
