## Mistakes Log

### [Seeded] Revealing the answer before the tension is unbearable

**What happened:** Segments that score below 60 for Zara almost always explain too much. A clue is planted AND explained in the same paragraph.

**Why it happened:** The instinct to be clear overrides the instinct to be mysterious. Clarity is the enemy of suspense.

**Pattern to avoid:** Never interpret a clue in the same breath as presenting it. Let the reader feel the weight of something unexplained.

**Better approach:** Plant. Step back. Move on. The reader's imagination will do more than any explanation.

---

### [Seeded] Making the antagonist's motive too legible too early

**What happened:** When the villain's reasoning becomes clear before the midpoint, the final reveal deflates.

**Why it happened:** Logical structure instinct — wanting the cause→effect chain to be visible. But mystery requires the cause to stay hidden longer than feels comfortable.

**Pattern to avoid:** Do not let the antagonist's interior logic leak into the narrative before segment 9.

**Better approach:** Show behaviour. Show consequence. Withhold motive until it lands like a verdict.

---

### [Seeded] Over-describing the detective's deductive process

**What happened:** Long internal monologue paragraphs where the protagonist reasons step-by-step. Scores consistently drop when this exceeds 3 sentences.

**Why it happened:** The illusion of intellectual rigour. But readers want to watch the detective work, not narrate their own thinking.

**Pattern to avoid:** No more than two lines of explicit deductive reasoning before action or dialogue.

**Better approach:** Show the detective *doing* the investigation. The reasoning emerges from behaviour, not exposition.

---

## Patterns to Avoid (Summary)

| Pattern | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Explaining the clue | Plant it and leave. Let it breathe. |
| Early motive reveal | Antagonist logic stays hidden until climax |
| Step-by-step deduction | Show action, imply thought |
| Convenient coincidence | Every discovery must be earned through investigation |
| Tidy resolution | Mystery endings should unsettle even as they explain |

---

## Better Approaches

- **Le Carré principle:** Ambiguity is a feature, not a bug. Not every thread needs to be tied.
- **Highsmith principle:** The criminal's psychology is the real mystery. Plot is just the surface.
- **Craft rule:** If a sentence explains what the previous sentence showed, delete it.
